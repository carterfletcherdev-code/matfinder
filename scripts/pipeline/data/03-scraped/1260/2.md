 ## What Actually Changes in Your Child

 The physical part is obvious. Stronger. More coordinated. Better body awareness. That happens naturally through consistent training.
 What parents do not always expect is everything else.
 Focus. Jiu jitsu demands full attention. You cannot think about something else when you are learning a new technique or working with a partner. That level of engagement, practiced two or three times a week, builds a habit of focus that children carry into school, into homework, into every situation that requires them to pay attention.
 Emotional regulation. The mat is one of the only places a child regularly encounters difficulty in a structured, supervised setting with a coach who knows how to guide them through it. Children who train consistently get better at handling frustration. Not because they are told to. Because they practice it, repeatedly, in a safe environment where the consequence of struggling is learning, not failure.
 Confidence that is earned. Not the kind that comes from being told you are great. The kind that comes from doing something hard and figuring it out. That is a different feeling entirely. Children who earn their confidence on the mat know the difference and it shows.
 Respect that is real. The culture at 2nd Gear is built on it. Coaches model it. Students practice it. It becomes the standard the child holds themselves to, on the mat and off.

 ## What Your Child’s Class Actually Looks Like

 A lot of parents spend more time wondering what the first class will feel like than it actually takes to go through it.
 Here is exactly what happens.
 Warm Up The class starts with movement. Energy gets focused. Kids get their bodies ready and their minds into the room. The warm up is structured so every child knows what to expect from the moment they walk on the mat.
 Technique The coach introduces a specific technique and breaks it down step by step. Not just what to do but why it works and how it connects to what they already know. Children at every level work through the same material at an age-appropriate pace.
 Partner Drills Kids apply what they just learned with a supervised partner. This is where the technique becomes real. The coach watches every pair and adjusts in real time. No child is left to figure it out alone.
 Skill Games and Reinforcement Learning is reinforced through structured games that feel like play and function like drilling. Kids stay engaged because the work stays interesting.
 Close Every class ends the same way. With respect. Students line up, coaches acknowledge the work, and the standard that started the class finishes it the same way.
 The routine is intentional. Children who know what to expect feel safe enough to take risks. That is when the real learning happens.

 ## Programs by Age

 #### Kids Jiu Jitsu (Ages 5–7) At this age the goal is not technique mastery. It is building the habits that make technique possible. Listening when the coach is speaking. Staying present in the room. Working with a partner in a controlled way. Moving their body with intention. These are the foundations of everything that comes later and they are built deliberately in every class.
 Progress at this age looks like a child who lines up without being told twice. Who tries again when something does not work. Who helps a partner up without thinking about it. Those moments matter more than any specific technique.

 #### Kids Jiu Jitsu (Ages 8–12) This is where jiu jitsu starts to become a real tool. Older kids are ready for problem solving, for being challenged, and for the kind of structured competition that teaches them what they are capable of. They learn to apply technique under pressure. They develop a training ethic. They start to take ownership of their own improvement in a way younger children cannot yet.
 This age group benefits enormously from the specific kind of challenge jiu jitsu provides. Difficulty with a structure around it. A coach who pushes and a room that supports. That combination builds something in a child that most activities simply cannot replicate.

 #### Kids Muay Thai (Ages 7–13) Muay Thai at this age is about movement, coordination, and physical development. Children learn proper striking mechanics, footwork, and how to move their bodies with control and intention. All training is closely supervised and adapted to what each child is ready for.
 The confidence that comes from learning how to strike with proper form, in a safe and structured environment, is real and it is specific. Children who train Muay Thai move differently. They stand differently. They know something about themselves that they did not know before.
